Cognitive Biases
are not random quirks.

201 documented biases — each one a systematic error in how the brain handles information, meaning, action, and memory. They're organized here by BIM pillar: the layer of consumer psychology where each bias does most of its damage, or most of its work.

Not a list. A map.

Every bias textbook you've ever read treats these as a collection of curiosities. That's the wrong frame. Biases are organized by what they protect. The brain can't process everything — so it built shortcuts. Those shortcuts cluster around four problems: too much information, not enough meaning, the need to act fast, and what to remember. The BIM framework maps those problems to the layer of consumer psychology where they surface. Once you see the structure, you stop treating biases as one-off tactics and start using them as diagnostic tools.

Cognitive Drivers 94 biases

The brain's noise filter
runs on bad math.

Nearly half the bias database lives here — and it makes sense. Cognitive biases are the brain doing its job: cutting the information load down to something manageable. The problem is the shortcuts it uses.

The Cognitive Drivers pillar governs how people filter information, assign meaning, and form the beliefs that drive decisions. 40 of the 94 biases here are meaning-making errors — the brain filling in gaps, finding patterns that don't exist, and projecting past experience onto new situations. Another 27 cluster around action: heuristics that let the brain commit to a decision without spending resources on full evaluation.

Most brands default to Cognitive messaging: features, benefits, logical arguments, price comparisons. That's not wrong — but it's playing on the surface. The deeper Cognitive lever is how you frame the information, not which information you share. Endowment, escalation of commitment, self-consistency — these don't respond to more information. They respond to reference points, ownership moments, and the story the consumer is already telling themselves.

Self-consistency Bias  Cog-105
66 transitions

People maintain a self-image of consistency — they remember their past choices as more aligned with their current beliefs than they actually were. This makes loyalty feel inevitable rather than chosen. And it makes any brand message that challenges that self-image feel like an attack.

Loyal · Repeat Conversion complexity 4/5 Not Enough Meaning
Endowment Effect  Cog-141
43 transitions

Once someone owns something — even briefly, even a free trial — they value it more than they would to acquire it. The psychological ownership precedes the transaction. Free trials aren't marketing tactics. They're endowment activation.

Repeat Conversion · Loyal complexity 2/5 Need to Act Fast
Escalation of Commitment  Cog-132
147 transitions

The most-linked bias in the database. People continue investing in decisions because of what they've already spent — time, money, identity. Churn protection, upsell resistance, and competitive switching all have Escalation of Commitment somewhere in the mechanism.

Repeat Conversion · Loyal complexity 3/5 Need to Act Fast
What most brands miss BIM Pillar Mismatch

Cognitive Drivers get the most attention — and the shallowest application. Brands use cognitive tools (features, logic, price proof) to fight emotional problems. A customer who's losing confidence in their purchase didn't need more data. They needed a story about who they are. The BIM diagnostic failure here is mistaking the symptom (low engagement, low conversion) for a Cognitive problem when the active driver is Self-consistency or Loss Aversion — both of which require Emotional or narrative intervention, not additional evidence.

Environment & Design 59 biases

Context is doing more work
than your copy is.

The biases in this pillar don't live in the consumer's head — they live in the environment you build around them. And they fire whether you designed for them or not.

Environment & Design governs how context, order, contrast, and framing shape decisions without the consumer being aware of it. The Availability Heuristic (Cog-001, 333 usage events) and Framing Effect (Cog-024, 59 events) are the two highest-use biases in the entire database — both live here.

This is the pillar where choice architecture operates. What's shown first, what's shown alongside what, what's absent, what's repeated — these are all active design decisions whether brands treat them as such or not. A brand that hasn't consciously designed its choice environment has still designed one. The biases are running on whatever defaults exist.

Availability Heuristic  Cog-001
333 usage events

What's easy to recall feels more likely, more common, more important. Reach and frequency aren't just media KPIs — they're the mechanism that makes your brand feel like the obvious choice when the consumer is standing at a decision point you're not in the room for.

Awareness · Repeat Conversion complexity 2/5 Too Much Information
Framing Effect  Cog-024
59 usage events

The same information presented differently produces different decisions. "95% fat-free" and "5% fat" describe identical products. The frame — not the fact — is what lands emotionally. Every message is framed. The question is whether you chose the frame or defaulted to one.

All stages complexity 2/5 Too Much Information
Anchoring Effect  Cog-019
21 usage events

The first number seen becomes the reference for every number that follows. Pricing strategy, discount presentation, and value comparison all run on anchoring. Showing the original price before the sale price is not a formatting choice — it's an anchor being set.

Awareness · 1st Conversion complexity 1/5 Too Much Information
What most brands miss SAEO · Application Layer

Environment & Design is where default settings become strategy failures. The SAEO Application layer asks: are your behavioral tools actually deployed in the right context, at the right moment, in the right sequence? Most brands that understand Framing still don't apply it consistently — different teams own different touchpoints and each defaults to their own frame. The diagnostic failure is treating E&D as a creative concern (design team's job) rather than a behavioral architecture decision that requires coordination across every customer-facing surface.

Social Influence 33 biases

People decide individually.
But not in isolation.

Social Influence biases don't require a crowd. They require a reference group — real, implied, or imagined. And consumers are almost always referencing one.

The Social Influence pillar governs how authority, identity, group norms, and perceived consensus shape individual decisions. Authority Bias (Cog-064) has the highest usage count in this pillar at 45 events — which means it surfaces most frequently in the liminal moments where consumers need permission to believe.

Social biases are permission structures. A consumer who is already considering your product is looking for a signal that the decision is sanctioned — by someone credible, by their peer group, or by the evidence of widespread adoption. The brand that provides that signal at the right moment converts. The brand that doesn't loses to whatever reference the consumer finds on their own.

Authority Bias  Cog-064
45 usage events · 12 transitions

People weight expert opinion disproportionately — not because they've verified the expertise, but because authority is a credibility shortcut. Influencer marketing, certifications, academic citations, and "as seen in" placement all run on Authority Bias. The mechanism isn't persuasion. It's permission.

Awareness · 1st Conversion complexity 1/5 Not Enough Meaning
Reciprocity Bias  Cog-198
16 transitions

The obligation to return a favor is near-universal across cultures. Free content, free tools, samples, and lead magnets aren't just generosity — they're reciprocity triggers. The mechanism: give first, unconditionally, and the social contract does the follow-up.

Awareness · Conversion · Advocacy complexity 1/5 Need to Act Fast
Trait Ascription Bias  Cog-123
43 transitions

People see themselves as flexible and nuanced, and see others — including competitors and brands — as fixed and predictable. The brand that positions itself as attuned to individual complexity wins against the brand that makes assumptions about who its customers are.

Loyal · Indecisive complexity 3/5 Need to Act Fast
What most brands miss Marketing Maturity Gap

Social Influence biases require sourcing, not just messaging. It's not enough to know Authority Bias is active at first conversion — you have to have built the authority signals that can be deployed at that moment. That's a pre-campaign capability question, not a creative execution question. Most brands discover they need social proof when they're already in-market and don't have it. That's a Marketing Maturity failure: the capability should exist before the campaign needs it.

Emotional Drivers 15 biases

The smallest pillar.
The loudest decisions.

Only 15 biases — but Loss Aversion alone links to 50 liminal transitions. Emotional Drivers aren't common. They're intense. And they move at inflection points, not at awareness stages.

Emotional Drivers govern fear of loss, regret, risk, urgency, and identity protection. They are the most powerful biases in the database by impact-per-event — and the most commonly misapplied. Brands reach for emotional levers at Awareness, when most Emotional biases are active at Retention, Churn Risk, and Re-engagement.

Loss Aversion (Cog-134) is the canonical example. Kahneman and Tversky established the 2.5× pain-to-gain asymmetry — losses hurt roughly twice as much as equivalent gains feel good. That asymmetry doesn't just inform pricing and urgency tactics. It structures the entire retention layer of the consumer journey.

Loss Aversion  Cog-134
50 transitions · 34 usage events

Losses feel 2.5× more painful than equivalent gains feel good. Every retention campaign, churn intervention, urgency trigger, and free trial conversion is operating on this mechanism — whether the brand knows it or not.

Repeat Conversion · Loyal complexity 1/5 Need to Act Fast
Status Quo Bias  Cog-148
65 transitions

Change feels risky even when staying is worse. Incumbents don't win on merit — they win on the emotional cost of switching. Every challenger brand is fighting Status Quo Bias before it's fighting any competitive advantage.

Loyal · Lapsed complexity 4/5 Need to Act Fast
Sunk Cost Fallacy  Cog-130
Emotional Drivers

Consumers continue with a product or relationship because of what they've already invested, not because of current value. This is a retention mechanism that works in your favor — until it doesn't, and the customer cancels with resentment instead of satisfaction.

Repeat Conversion · Loyal complexity 4/5 Need to Act Fast
What most brands miss BIM Pillar Mismatch

Emotional Drivers are the last place most brands apply emotional language, and the first place they should. The failure pattern: Awareness campaigns are flooded with emotional creative (fear, aspiration, humor) while Retention and Re-engagement campaigns fall back to rational messaging — "here's what you get with your subscription." But Loss Aversion, Status Quo Bias, and Sunk Cost are all active at Retention. The customer who's considering canceling needs an emotional intervention, not a feature list.
