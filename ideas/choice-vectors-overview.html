Good Assumptions · Behavioral Intelligence

Choice Vectors
are not tactics.

343 documented behavioral forces — each one the psychological mechanism that makes a tactic work, or explains why it doesn't. Organized here by BIM pillar and by what they do to a decision moment: accelerate it, stabilize it, arrest its decay, or redirect it toward a better outcome.

The distinction that matters

A tactic is a thing you do. A vector is why that thing moves behavior. "Limited-time offer" is a tactic. Loss Aversion activated by temporal pressure is the vector. When a tactic fails, brands ask "what different tactic should we try?" The better question is: which vector was supposed to be active, and did the conditions for it actually exist? 343 vectors map to 160 liminal transitions — the moments in a consumer journey where behavior can shift. That's the diagnostic architecture. Vectors are the mechanism layer between the consumer's psychology and your execution.

Cognitive Drivers 118 vectors

The largest pillar.
The most misapplied one.

118 vectors govern how information is framed, simplified, and encoded into decision-making. Brands default here because rational messaging feels safe. But most Cognitive vectors aren't about giving people more information — they're about changing the reference point the information is measured against.

The Cognitive Drivers pillar covers framing, anchoring, simplification, memory strategies, and choice architecture. 46 of the 118 are framing and anchoring vectors — the largest single cluster — which means the most-used behavioral tool in marketing is reference-point manipulation. Every price display, every comparison chart, every before/after narrative is running on Cognitive framing.

The failure mode: brands apply Cognitive vectors to Emotional problems. A customer who's losing confidence in their decision doesn't need a clearer information hierarchy. They need a story about what their decision says about them. Cognitive vectors work at Consideration and Decision stages. At Retention and Re-engagement, the active pillar almost always shifts.

Change Framing  CV-024
59 transitions · Accelerate

Communicates the positive side of behavior change or competitor switching by reframing disruption as opportunity. Based on Prospect Theory: framing change as gain rather than loss reduces psychological resistance and activates approach motivation instead of avoidance. Every challenger brand needs this vector before it needs anything else.

Consideration stage difficulty 6/10 Cognitive Framing & Anchoring
Streamlined Onboarding  CV-320
50 transitions · Accelerate

Optimizes first product contact by minimizing cognitive load and engineering early wins. Based on Serial Position Effect and progressive learning theory — initial experience quality determines whether the Endowment Effect activates. Poor onboarding means the trial period never creates psychological ownership. The trial fails before it starts.

Retention stage difficulty 6/10 Simplification & Complexity Reduction
Cognitive Dissonance Resolution  CV-339
36 transitions · Stabilize

Addresses the tension between a customer's positive past experience and current dissatisfaction by providing frameworks that separate temporary problems from fundamental value. Based on Festinger's dissonance theory — when this vector is absent at product failure moments, customers don't just churn. They become vocal detractors.

Retention stage difficulty 5/10 Cognitive Framing & Anchoring
What most brands miss SAEO · Application Layer

Cognitive vectors require sequence, not just presence. Change Framing at Consideration only works if Streamlined Onboarding activates the Endowment Effect at first use, and Cognitive Dissonance Resolution is queued for the first friction moment. Most brands deploy these independently, campaign by campaign, without designing the vector chain. The SAEO Application layer diagnostic question is: which vectors are active at each stage, and are they sequenced to reinforce each other — or deployed in isolation where they lose half their effect?

Emotional Drivers 88 vectors

88 vectors.
The highest average difficulty.

Emotional vectors are the hardest to execute and the most powerful when they land. Difficulty score averages 5.9/10 across the pillar — the highest of the four. They require organizational readiness that most teams haven't built before they need it.

The Emotional Drivers pillar covers Loss & Risk Communication (24 vectors), Identity & Ownership Psychology (21 vectors), Motivation & Empowerment (16), Emotional Influence & Mood Management (14), and Future Visualization (13). Identity is the second-largest framework cluster at 21 vectors — which means nearly a quarter of Emotional vectors operate on who the consumer believes they are, not what they feel in the moment.

Continued Commitment (CV-047) and Relationship Investment Amplification (CV-342) are the two most-linked vectors in the entire database at 77 transitions each. Both are Emotional. Both operate on the same mechanism: making the accumulated investment visible enough that leaving feels like losing something real. The vector with the most reach in the database is not a tactic. It's an identity structure.

Continued Commitment  CV-047
77 transitions · Stabilize

Surfaces previous consumer investment — time, data, decisions — to encourage ongoing engagement. Based on Commitment Escalation and Sunk Cost research. The mechanism: people continue investments to justify what they've already spent, and consistency with past actions reduces cognitive dissonance. This vector is the #1 linked vector in the database. It's everywhere in the Retention stage because it works.

Retention stage difficulty 6/10 Commitment & Consistency · Social Influence
Switching Cost Amplification  CV-327
54 transitions · Arrest

Quantifies the total economic and psychological cost of leaving — setup complexity, migration risk, learning curves, data loss, opportunity cost — making churn feel expensive before the consumer has done the math. The hardest implementation vector in this tier at difficulty 9/10. Deployed wrong, it reads as a threat. Deployed right, it reads as honesty.

Reactivation stage difficulty 9/10 Loss Aversion & Risk Communication
Identity Threat Response  CV-329
52 transitions · Arrest

Frames potential churn as inconsistent with who the consumer believes they are — their values, their role, their self-image. Based on Identity Protection Bias and Social Identity Theory. When a fitness brand says "people like you don't quit," that's Identity Threat Response. It's also the vector most at risk of backfiring when deployed inauthentically.

Reactivation stage difficulty 6/10 Identity & Ownership Psychology
What most brands miss BIM Pillar Mismatch

Emotional vectors get deployed at the wrong stage in two ways. First, brands flood Awareness with emotional creative (fear, aspiration, humor) while Retention campaigns revert to rational messaging — feature lists and usage stats. But the highest-impact Emotional vectors are Retention and Reactivation instruments, not Awareness ones. Second, brands apply Identity vectors to consumers who haven't yet formed an identity connection with the brand. Identity Threat Response requires prior identity alignment to exist. Deployed at first conversion, it reads as presumptuous. Deployed at month 6, it reads as deeply known. Timing is the variable most teams skip in the planning brief.

Environment & Design 86 vectors

Vectors that work
whether you designed for them or not.

Environment & Design vectors operate on the context around a decision, not the content of it. Feedback systems, timing, visual salience, and habit formation are active at every touchpoint — and they're running on whatever defaults you've built, intentionally or not.

The E&D pillar's largest cluster is Feedback & Reinforcement Systems (25 vectors) — which tells you something important: the behavioral architecture of your product, how and when it tells consumers they're doing the right thing, is the highest-leverage design surface in this pillar. Timing & Contextual Triggers (17 vectors) is next. The when matters as much as the what.

Post-purchase Communication (CV-196) is the most-linked E&D vector with 38 transition links — and it's one of the most systematically neglected. Brands invest heavily in pre-purchase persuasion and almost nothing in post-purchase reinforcement. But post-purchase is where buyer's remorse, self-consistency maintenance, and the foundation for repeat conversion all live.

Post-purchase Communication  CV-196
38 transitions · Stabilize

Strategic engagement after the transaction to reinforce the decision, reduce buyer's remorse, and build loyalty through ongoing value demonstration. Based on choice-supportive bias and relationship maintenance theory. The consumer who feels good about their decision at day 3 is three times more likely to still be a customer at month 6.

Retention stage difficulty 5/10 Feedback & Reinforcement Systems
Temporal Perspective Reframing  CV-343
40 transitions · Stabilize

Shifts focus from immediate frustrations to longer-term value trajectories. Based on Hershfield's research on future-self connection and temporal discounting. Consumers undervalue future benefits relative to immediate costs — this vector makes future outcomes vivid and personally relevant enough to compete with present pain.

Retention stage difficulty 6/10 Timing & Contextual Triggers
Initial Offer  CV-142
48 transitions · Accelerate

Reduces perceived purchase risk through introductory pricing, creating an anchored reference point and beginning Endowment Effect activation before the full commitment. The mechanism isn't generosity — it's lowering the decision threshold so the consumer crosses it and psychological ownership begins.

Decision stage difficulty 6/10 Choice Architecture & Defaults
What most brands miss Marketing Maturity Gap

Environment & Design vectors require cross-functional ownership that most org structures don't have. Post-purchase Communication sits at the intersection of marketing, product, and CX. Temporal Perspective Reframing requires the product to surface data that most analytics stacks don't capture. Initial Offer requires pricing authority that marketing rarely controls. The maturity failure here is structural — these vectors can't be deployed campaign by campaign. They require persistent behavioral architecture that most companies haven't built because no single team owns the full sequence.

Social Influence 51 vectors

The smallest pillar.
Built before the campaign needs it.

Social Influence vectors are permission structures — signals that tell the consumer their decision is sanctioned by someone credible, a group they belong to, or the weight of evidence. You can't build these in-campaign. They're infrastructure, not creative.

Social Proof & Community (21 vectors) and Authority & Credibility (19 vectors) account for nearly 80% of the Social Influence pillar. Both operate on the same fundamental mechanism: borrowed conviction. The consumer who isn't certain looks for a signal that others are. The consumer who's skeptical looks for a source whose skepticism has already been resolved.

Trust & Transparency (CV-302) is the Social Influence vector with the deepest link structure across VALIDATION, RECOVERY, and CRISIS functional categories — meaning it's relevant not just at acquisition but across the entire trust arc of the customer journey. Most brands deploy it at Awareness. Its highest-impact applications are at Recovery and Re-engagement.

Trust & Transparency  CV-302
Accelerate · Prevent · Recovery

Openly sharing pricing, processes, policies, and product realities to reduce skepticism and build credibility. Trust & Transparency links to LIM-011 (Skepticism → Trust), LIM-147 (Post-Crisis → Trust Rebuilt), and eight exit/crisis transitions where it functions as the prevention mechanism. Everlane built $100M in revenue on radical cost transparency. Domino's used it to reverse a brand crisis.

Consideration · Recovery stages difficulty 5/10 Authority & Credibility
Social Investment Highlighting  CV-345
30 transitions · Arrest

Makes visible the social connections, community status, and relationships that a consumer would lose by leaving — networks, contributions, reputation, shared achievements. Based on Robert Putnam's social capital research. The mechanism isn't FOMO. It's that social investments feel genuinely non-transferable.

Retention stage difficulty 8/10 Social Proof & Community
Continued Commitment  CV-047
77 transitions · Stabilize

Technically filed under Social Influence / Commitment & Consistency, but operates across all four pillars in practice. It's listed here because its mechanism is fundamentally social: people are consistent with their public commitments and past decisions because inconsistency is socially costly, not just cognitively uncomfortable.

Retention stage difficulty 6/10 Commitment & Consistency
What most brands miss BIM Pillar Mismatch

Social Influence vectors can't be manufactured at deployment time. Authority Bias requires existing credibility signals. Social Proof requires real evidence of adoption. Trust & Transparency requires a history of authentic disclosure. The brand that discovers it needs Social Influence vectors when the campaign is already live has already lost the timing window. The diagnostic failure here is a planning gap — Social Influence infrastructure belongs in the 12-month brand capability plan, not in the campaign brief. Most teams invert this: they plan the creative first and look for the credibility signals second. That's why influencer partnerships get bolted on last, social proof modules get populated with weak reviews, and transparency efforts feel performative. The vector requires the asset. Build the asset first.
