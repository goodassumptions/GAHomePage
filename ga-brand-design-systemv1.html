01 Foundation

Principles

Good Assumptions is a behavioral marketing intelligence brand. Every design decision earns the right to exist by serving comprehension — not performing credibility.

01
Evidence before
elegance

Every claim is grounded in behavioral science. Design signals rigor — not aspiration. Skepticism is the default stance, earned confidence the target.

02
Precision over
decoration

Visual hierarchy communicates information structure. Accent colors carry semantic weight. Nothing is decorative — if it doesn't communicate, remove it.

03
Calm intelligence

The tone is never urgent, never alarmist, never sales-y. The reader is treated as a practitioner — someone doing real work, not a prospect to be converted.

04
Accessible at
every layer

Text contrast meets WCAG AA minimum throughout. Color is never the sole carrier of meaning — every color signal is paired with type or shape context.

Reasoning Modes

At every threshold moment, abductive reasoning dominates. These three modes are color-coded in SAEO's causal reasoning maps and chat responses.

Abductive
Inference to the best explanation. The universal mode at threshold moments — "what's the most likely cause of what I'm seeing?"
Inductive
Pattern-based generalization from observed data. Used in post-transition consolidation and behavioral pattern recognition.
Deductive
Rule-application from established principles. Used in tactical recommendation — once the transition type is confirmed.

✓ Do
Use weight 300 for display prose and long-form body. It signals considered intelligence, not casualness. Let spacing and size carry hierarchy.
✗ Don't
Don't bold headlines for visual impact. Don't use italic for anything other than genuine editorial emphasis or the display font's character style.
✓ Do
Always use uppercase + letter-spacing for eyebrow labels and metadata. This is the only sanctioned use of all-caps — never in body copy.
✗ Don't
Don't use text-3 color (#6a6860) for any text that carries meaning. Only decorative chrome, separators, and background label elements.

08 Voice

Voice & Writing

Good Assumptions writes for practitioners, not prospects. The voice is precise, earned, occasionally blunt. Never explanatory for its own sake. Never self-congratulatory.

Four voice attributes
Precise
Specific over general. Use behavioral science terms correctly. Name the mechanism. Don't hedge with "might" when the evidence says "does."
Earned skepticism
We cite evidence or we stay quiet. Claims are grounded. GenX epistemic foundation — show your work, acknowledge limitations.
Calm authority
Never urgent. Never alarmist. The intelligence should feel like talking to a very well-read colleague who has no reason to sell you anything.
Direct
Shortest path to the insight. No preamble. No "great question." No "as we can see." Start with the claim, follow with the evidence.
Writing examples
✗ Don't write this
It's really important that marketers understand how consumer psychology plays a role in the exciting journey through the funnel.
✓ Write this instead
Funnel stages don't exist as psychological states. Consumers transition between discrete behavioral modes — and the transition itself is where brands lose.
✗ Reframe labels
Understanding cognitive biases can help you better connect with customers.
✓ Reframe labels
This isn't about understanding your audience better. It's about understanding what your audience's nervous system is optimized to do — and whether your brand is working with it or against it.
Headline formulas
The Naming Pattern
"The [Named Concept]" — implies taxonomy, earned classification. Used for liminal states and defined phenomena.
"The Trust Threshold" · "The Sunk Cost Window"
The Reframe
Challenges a received wisdom in the first sentence. Sets up the behavioral science counter-explanation.
"This isn't a loyalty problem. It's a consolidation failure."
The Precise Claim
No hedge, no softening. States what the evidence says directly. The confidence signals credibility.
"Funnel stages don't describe psychological states. They describe marketer preferences."
The Weight of Why
Opens with the consumer's psychological state before the brand enters the picture. Establishes the prior question.
"What a consumer needs to move — and whether this brand has standing to provide it."
