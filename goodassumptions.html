About Good Assumptions
About Good Assumptions

Marketing is the science of understanding why people do what they do — and designing for it.

Most marketing doesn't fail because the team wasn't good enough. It fails because the wrong problem got solved at the wrong layer. Good Assumptions exists to change that.

We are a behavioral marketing intelligence practice. We connect the academic science of human decision-making to the practical problems practitioners face every day — and we built a platform to do it at scale.

Our definition

What marketing actually is

The classic definition — right product, right person, right time — was built for a linear world. Consumers don't move linearly anymore. The journey is fragmented. The decision isn't a moment; it's a threshold.

"Marketing is the art and science of understanding, guiding, and nudging human behavior through meaningful interactions that build lasting relationships between brands and consumers across every touchpoint of the journey."

— Good Assumptions working definition

The consumer is not a target to be reached. They are a person moving through psychological states — and every state has its own logic, its own friction, its own opportunity.

Old model
Right message, right channel, right time
Assumes a rational consumer moving predictably through a funnel. Optimizes for reach and frequency. Treats behavior as the output of exposure.
Good Assumptions model
Right understanding, right threshold, right intervention
Assumes a human moving through psychological transitions. Optimizes for decision-moment alignment. Treats behavior as the output of context, cognition, and design.
How we think

Our goals in every engagement

Five things. In order. The sequence matters — each one creates the conditions for the next.

01
Understand the customer through empathy and data
Not demographics. Behavioral profiles. What they believe, what they fear, what they're in the middle of deciding.
02
Anticipate needs at critical moments of the journey
Threshold moments — the liminal points where a consumer transitions from one psychological state to the next — are where marketing either earns trust or loses it.
03
Influence decisions using choice architecture and behavioral intelligence
Kahneman, Thaler, Norman — the research exists. The work is connecting it to the practitioner's actual problem.
04
Create frictionless experiences across every touchpoint
Digital, physical, or somewhere in between. Friction is a behavioral failure before it's a UX failure.
05
Build relationships beyond loyalty — toward advocacy and cultural relevance
Loyalty is a behavior. Advocacy is an identity. The difference is whether the brand has become part of how the consumer sees themselves.

The behavioral science

BIM: the four pillars of human decision-making

Six decades of behavioral economics and design research. Four questions about every consumer decision.

Cognitive drivers

How people process information and evaluate options. Loss aversion, anchoring, endowment effect, effort justification. The mental shortcuts that shape every choice before the consumer is consciously aware of them.

Emotional drivers

How feelings and psychological states influence decisions. Peak-end rule, IKEA effect, framing, FOMO. Emotion is not decoration on top of a rational decision — it is the decision, with reason applied after the fact.

Social drivers

How peers, norms, and community dynamics shape behavior. Social proof, authority bias, bandwagon effect, scarcity perception. People follow others especially in moments of uncertainty — exactly where marketing intervenes.

Environment & design

How physical and digital contexts nudge decisions before conscious thought. Default effect, choice overload, decoy effect, temporal discounting. The design of a choice environment is itself a form of messaging.

"We think, each of us, that we're much more rational than we are."

— Daniel Kahneman
