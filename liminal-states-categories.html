Validation

Trust is being decided right now — and your next move will either resolve it or close it off.

The consumer is deciding whether to believe you. Not whether to buy. Validation transitions fire at first contact, mid-evaluation, post-purchase, and mid-relationship. Trust is always being decided somewhere — and most brands only instrument the moments they can see.

Health Inertia · The Stuck Status QuoExit
"Why do people who want to be healthier keep not starting?" — Intention is real. The current state is comfortable enough.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers · ROI 9
Buyer's Remorse Window · The Doubting WindowCrisis
"What happens when buyers second-guess their purchase?" — Cognitive dissonance peaks the moment the decision is made. ROI 10.
BIM: Emotional Drivers
What most brands miss BIM + Liminal
The Buyer's Remorse Window opens after the sale — which most brands treat as a handoff to operations. But it's still a Validation transition. Emotional Drivers are running the show. Brands that respond with feature confirmations make the dissonance worse. The correct move is identity reinforcement: you made the right call, here's proof from people like you.

Deepening

The relationship is either growing or quietly eroding — and most brands stop paying attention right when it matters most.

The largest category. 28 of 37 transitions are exits. Post-purchase is where most brands go quiet — and where most relationships actually fall apart. The exits don't announce themselves. Silence reads as retention. The data says otherwise.

Satisfied → Loyal · The Identity CommitmentForward
"Why do high-satisfaction customers still switch when a better deal appears?" — Satisfaction is a score. Loyalty is identity.
BIM: Emotional Drivers · ROI 9
ROI Doubt · The Uncertain InvestmentExit
"When the math starts to feel wrong" — Delivering on the promise isn't enough if they can't articulate the value.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers · ROI 9
Complaint Entrenchment · The Permanent GrievanceCrisis
"When a customer's complaint becomes their identity" — No longer a request for resolution. A story they tell about who they are.
BIM: Emotional Drivers
What most brands miss SAEO Diagnostic
ROI Doubt is consistently misdiagnosed as an Execution problem — better reporting, clearer dashboards. But it's a Strategy problem. The brand never defined what "value" would look like for this customer before they bought. The SAEO diagnostic catches this: the symptom is in Execution, the failure is in Strategy Layer 03.

Commitment

The consumer is deciding whether to go further — and the psychology of going further is nothing like the psychology of getting started.

Commitment transitions are about escalating investment. The forward transitions here carry some of the highest ROI scores in the database. The exits are quieter — upgrade paths that stop converting, trials that expire without a decision.

Trial → Paid · The Payment LeapForward
"When do free users decide to pay?" — The moment product-market fit becomes paying-customer-fit. ROI 10.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers
Scale Paralysis · The Complexity CeilingExit
"Why do organizations with successful pilots still fail to roll out?" — Not decision fatigue. Organizational comprehension failure.
BIM: Environment & Design · ROI 7
Hollow Compliance Persistence · Surface Compliance, Absent ConvictionPost-res
"If we removed the incentive today, would this consumer repeat the behavior tomorrow?" — Metrics positive. Commitment absent.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers · ROI 7
What most brands miss BIM Pillar mismatch
Hollow Compliance looks like retention on every dashboard. But the BIM pillar driving the behavior is Environment & Design — inertia, sunk cost, defaults — not genuine commitment. When a competitor removes switching friction, these customers leave in clusters and nobody sees it coming.

Crisis

Something is actively breaking down — and the window to catch it is measured in hours, not campaign cycles.

The highest average ROI category. Brands that intervene correctly here often end up with stronger relationships than they had before. The Service Recovery Paradox lives in this category. So does Active → Churn — the only terminal transition in the database.

Dormant → Reactivated · The Comeback HookForward
"What brings dormant customers back to life?" — New context, not better messaging, is usually what reopens the door. ROI 7.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers
Payment Threshold Stall · The Zero CrossingExit
"Why do consumers who love the free product fail to pay?" — The $0-to-$X transition triggers a pain response. The act of paying is the barrier, not the price. ROI 9.
BIM: Environment & Design
Private → Public Complaint · The Reputation BombCrisis
"What turns private complaints into public crises?" — One unhappy customer becomes a warning to thousands. ROI 10.
BIM: Social Influence · Extreme intensity
What most brands miss BIM + SAEO Diagnostic
The Reputation Bomb is treated as a PR problem — an Execution failure. But the BIM pillar is Social Influence at extreme intensity. The consumer's primary need is to be witnessed, not fixed. Brands that lead with resolution messaging accelerate the escalation. The SAEO diagnostic locates this as an Application failure: right insight, wrong intervention layer.

Transformation

The consumer's life just changed. Their old relationship with your category may not fit who they're becoming.

Life transitions rewrite identity. Parenthood, relocation, diagnosis, career shift, homeownership. Transformation transitions create large windows of brand openness. They close faster than most acquisition strategies can move.

Pre-Parent → Parent · The Everything Changes MomentForward
"How does becoming a parent change everything?" — Priorities, spending, identity — reorganized simultaneously. ROI 10.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers · Extreme intensity
Diagnosis Denial · The Identity RefusalExit
"What does it look like when someone refuses to become a patient?" — The identity cost of accepting the new reality is too high.
BIM: Emotional Drivers · Extreme intensity
Identity Mismatch · The Relevance GapCrisis
"When the brand you relied on stops feeling like it's for you" — The consumer changed. The brand didn't notice.
BIM: Emotional Drivers
What most brands miss Persona Journey + BIM
Transformation transitions don't respond to product messaging. They respond to identity acknowledgment. Leading with features at a life transition moment is applying Cognitive logic to an Emotional and Social driver moment. The consumer isn't evaluating your product — they're asking whether your brand understands who they're becoming.

Amplification

Private satisfaction is deciding whether to go public — and the only direction this category moves is forward or not at all.

Every transition in Amplification is a forward movement. The failure mode isn't an exit — it's the absence of a forward one. Silence. The satisfied customer who never shares. The influential fan who keeps the endorsement private. Most advocacy programs try to push. That's usually the move that kills organic advocacy.

All 12 transitions are forward movements. The three cards show the spectrum: the advocate who earns it, the one who stalls at the threshold, and the one who crosses because of exceptional recovery.
User → Advocate · The Champion ShiftForward
"What turns satisfied users into vocal champions?" — Identity is now tied to the product. They share because it reflects who they are. ROI 9.
BIM: Social Influence
Influence Resistance · The Reluctant VoiceForward — stalled
"Why do powerful fans refuse to use their influence for us?" — Genuine fan. Real reach. Won't deploy it. Endorsing costs social capital they've spent years building. ROI 9.
BIM: Social Influence
Complaint → Advocate · The Turnaround MiracleForward
"How do complainers become brand champions?" — The contrast between frustration and exceptional recovery creates advocacy satisfied customers rarely match. ROI 9.
BIM: Emotional Drivers · Extreme intensity
What most brands miss BIM — Social pillar, Cognitive intervention
Every Amplification transition is driven by Social Influence — but most advocacy programs apply Cognitive logic: referral bonuses, points, structured incentives. Incentivizing advocacy signals the brand doesn't believe the product earns it organically — which is precisely what kills the transition. You can't buy Social Influence. You can only earn the conditions for it, then get out of the way.

07 of 09 11 transitions

Recovery

Trust was broken. The question isn't whether you fixed the problem — it's whether the relationship survived the fix.

The highest average ROI category in the database. Exceptional recovery creates loyalty that pre-crisis customers rarely reach. The Service Recovery Paradox is real and documented here. So are the four post-resolution exit states that show exactly how brands blow it in the moment right after they think they've fixed everything.

Problem → Resolution · The Fix MomentForward
"What defines a truly resolved problem?" — Acknowledgment, speed, and generosity that signals genuine care. Fixing the issue is the minimum. ROI 9.
BIM: Emotional Drivers
Crisis Trust Collapse · The Permanent Damage AssessmentExit
"When does a brand crisis become permanent damage?" — Recovery attempts too slow, too thin, or contradicted by ongoing actions. The consumer concludes the crisis revealed something structural.
BIM: Emotional Drivers · ROI 7
Consolidation Window Abandonment · Resolution CollapsePost-res
"Did success fade after we backed off?" — Metrics went positive. Brand reduced intervention. Consumer regressed. "It worked and then stopped" is harder to recover from than "it never worked." ROI 9.
BIM: Emotional Drivers
What most brands miss SAEO Diagnostic — Operations
Consolidation Window Abandonment is almost always an Operations failure masquerading as a Strategy success. The strategy worked — consumer came back. But the operational system read "resolved" and stopped the intervention cadence. The SAEO diagnostic locates this at the Operations layer: the strategy was sound, but the infrastructure for holding a recovered relationship didn't exist.

Relationship

The dynamic between consumer and brand is maturing — or calcifying into something that looks like loyalty and isn't.

Identity-level shifts in how the consumer relates to the brand. The slowest transitions in the database. Hollow Retention lives here: technically retained, psychologically absent, invisible in every standard metric until it surfaces as a churn cohort.

Champion → Executive Sponsor · The Authority AssumptionForward
"Why does the person who loves your product most often fail to become the person who buys it?" — Believing in the product and betting your professional reputation on it are different commitments. ROI 9.
BIM: Social Influence · High intensity
Sponsorship Stall · The Authority DeflectionExit
"What does it look like when the person who loves your product won't buy it?" — Keeps advocating. Deflects commitment to a committee. Deal enters indefinite hold.
BIM: Social Influence · ROI 7
Hollow Retention · The Loyal StrangerPost-res
"Do they engage only minimally without advocacy?" — Subscription active. Psychologically absent. Sunk cost doing the work of loyalty. Invisible until the churn event arrives at scale. ROI 9.
BIM: Cognitive Drivers · Low intensity
What most brands miss Marketing Maturity + SAEO Operations
Detecting Hollow Retention requires behavioral signal infrastructure — engagement depth, advocacy monitoring, sentiment layering — that most organizations at Maturity Stage 1 or 2 don't have. The SAEO diagnostic locates this as an Operations failure: the brand can't see what's happening in the relationship layer until the churn cohort surfaces in the monthly report. By then, the retention number was never real.

Channel

The consumer is moving between modes of engagement — and the relationship either survives the move or it doesn't.

The smallest category, and the one most consistently misread as a technical problem. A consumer moving across a channel is also re-evaluating their relationship with the brand in a new context. Most brands track the channel event. Few track what happens to the relationship inside it.

Digital → Physical · The Offline BridgeForward
"What drives the shift from digital to physical?" — The in-store experience either confirms the brand the consumer already formed — or doesn't. ROI 7.
BIM: Environment & Design · Low intensity
Digital Resistance · The Analog HoldoutExit
"When going digital feels like losing something" — Not incapability. A belief that the digital format is inferior. The value was in the physical interaction itself.
BIM: Emotional Drivers · Medium intensity · ROI 7
Channel Drop-off · The Lost ThreadCrisis
"The consumer moved channels and the brand didn't follow" — Not disengagement from the brand. Disengagement from the mode of contact. The thread breaks quietly.
BIM: Environment & Design
What most brands miss BIM — Environment vs Emotional mismatch
Digital Resistance is almost always treated as an adoption problem — better UX, smoother onboarding. But the BIM pillar is Emotional, not Environmental. The consumer isn't struggling with the interface. They're grieving a relationship format they valued. UX optimization applied to an Emotional driver moment confirms the brand doesn't understand what the consumer is actually experiencing.

These nine categories cover the full arc of a consumer relationship. They overlap. They fire simultaneously. A consumer in Commitment is often also in Validation. A consumer in Recovery may also be approaching Amplification.

That's not a flaw in the model — it's the model. Real behavior isn't sequential. The intelligence that helps you respond to it shouldn't be either.
